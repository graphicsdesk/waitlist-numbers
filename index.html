The ‘Classroom Problem’: Frustrations over registration reach a fever pitch.

By Irie Sentner, Zachary Schermele, and Ayaan Ali

September 26, 2022

Photo by Ryan Newberger

When Gustavo Hernandez, SEAS ’26, applied to Columbia, he assumed he would be able to easily get into the classes of his choice. Like many undergraduates, he said exploring what he wanted to take was part and parcel of getting the most out of his college experience.

Those are expectations the University has struggled for decades to meet.

The “Classroom Problem,” as it was first called in a faculty committee report in 1998, refers to Columbia’s decades-long struggle to schedule courses at “reasonable times in comfortable, conveniently located, adequately maintained and appropriately sized, configured and equipped classrooms.”

It’s a problem that has long been exacerbated when departments try to schedule classes at the same peak times. But it’s become particularly acute this semester, according to classroom data and interviews with faculty members, administrators, and students.

A Spectator analysis of the classroom directory found that as of the Friday night after the first week of classes, 21 out of 30 required introductory courses for the 10 largest undergraduate majors within the Faculty of Arts and Sciences had waitlists, with 13 of those courses having waitlists of 20 students or longer. Five of those courses—four of which were Calculus 1 courses, the other being Calculus-Based Introduction to Statistics—had waitlists exceeding 50 percent of the available seats.

“This has never happened before, that I can remember,” Erik Gray, a professor of English and comparative literature, said.

Gray’s introductory English course, Critical Texts, Literary Methods, which he has taught since 2010, had 75 students enrolled and 20 on the waitlist.

“Having to turn away students that very first time,” Gray said of this semester’s introductory course, “was painful.”

The struggle to get into classes is often especially hard on first-year students, who are given the latest registration times of all undergraduates, while juniors and seniors often over-register for courses they inevitably drop.

“People joke about how it’s harder to get into classes than it was to get into the college itself,” Hernandez said.

Adrienne Chacón, BC ’26, said she resorted to cross-registering for “random classes” because of the challenges of Columbia’s registration.

“If I drop a class, I need to be able to have enough credits,” Chacón said.

Departments have responded to the increased demand for introductory courses in various ways. The mathematics department, for instance, created an entirely new section of Calculus 1 to accommodate the flooding waitlists.

“This reduced the pressure,” mathematics professor Amadou Bah said. “I was getting a lot of messages [from] students who wanted to know where they were on the waitlist.”

In the week following the add/drop period, enrollment stabilized as students solidified their schedules. Many course enrollments dropped below the original caps and some departments, such as mathematics and computer science, created more classes to accommodate demand. As of last Wednesday, though, six of the introductory courses for the top 10 undergraduate majors in FAS were at maximum enrollment or overenrolled, and at least one course—Introduction to Computer Science and Programming in Java, which is offered through the School of Engineering and Applied Science—has been offered in an online-only modality to accommodate demand.

Many students have found the registration process to be highly unpredictable. For example, Laritza Chena, CC ’26, got into Science of Psychology, an introductory course that meets the Core Curriculum science requirement, despite being 88th on the waitlist and being told by friends it would be “super hard to get into.”

“I thought there was no chance of me getting in, so I was very shocked,” Chena said.

One of the factors exacerbating this semester’s problems with registration is what has historically been referred to as “bunching,” which is when departments schedule many classes around the same peak times. Faculty committees have previously developed and refined rules to curb “bunching,” but not every course-offering unit always follows those guidelines.

Registration issues also come in the wake of last year’s enrollment uptick, which resulted in a roughly 9 percent increase—or about 800 more students—in the undergraduate population. Concerns about yield, or how many students who were accepted to Columbia would actually enroll, played into administrators’ decisions in the 2021-22 school year to admit more students than usual.

At the same time, an inquiry was underway into potentially increasing the number of undergraduates in Columbia College and SEAS. That effort, despite uncovering excess capacity in a number of courses and departments, ultimately resulted in a recommendation to postpone expansion until spring or fall 2025, when Uris Hall is set to fully reopen as the Lavine Center. A number of courses, including in the political science and physics departments, have taken up classroom space in the building this semester even as it continues to undergo renovations following the Business School’s move to Manhattanville in the spring.

The expansion inquiry unearthed a wealth of new information on classrooms and registration. Less than a quarter of Columbia College students are satisfied with the current course registration process, according to a Columbia College Student Council survey conducted last spring in conjunction with one of the expansion faculty committees.

Photo by Ryan Newberger

Another report found that the stress of the registration process keeps students from taking required classes in the Core Curriculum when it would “best suit their academic programs.” In particular, students face difficulty finding Global Core courses that work well with their schedules.

“We need more faculty. There’s no question. There’s not enough spots right now. I don’t think there’s any question about that. I think that the avenues are there to hire more faculty, at least adjuncts, for doing this kind of thing,” said psychology professor Patricia Lindemann, who teaches Science of Psychology.

Many students also register for Music Humanities and Art Humanities, only to drop them at the eleventh hour for other classes they would rather take. This practice creates a “perception of scarcity” and prevents other students who are behind them in the registration process from registering, the final report from the Core and student life working group says.

On June 7, the expansion steering committee issued its final report, which recommended redesigning registration “to be less stressful for students.” The effort will include, according to the report, “addressing problems of the commons vs. the value of individual choice” and “considering the impacts of over-registration, waitlisting, and capping practices as well as basic course availability.”

In a statement, a University spokesperson said the University is currently looking into potential enhancements to the registration process.

“Over the past several years, the Registrar’s Office has been very responsive to student and faculty feedback and made important improvements to the registration process,” the spokesperson said in the statement.

This year is not the first time concerns about registration and classroom space have been raised at Columbia. In 1998, the Krauss Morningside Classroom Committee first identified widespread agreement in FAS about the “Classroom Problem” and uncovered inefficiencies the committee said were “largely intrinsic to the rooms themselves and to the process by which we assign classes to them.”

A decade later, in 2011, former Executive Vice President of Arts and Sciences Nicholas Dirks formed the classroom committee to follow up on those findings. The committee ultimately established new class scheduling guidelines for departments in order to prevent “bunching,” when a lot of classes are scheduled around the same peak times.

Additionally, the 2011 report recommended adding two new lecture periods, more Friday seminars, and updating classroom technology, which was intended to limit competition between faculty for more high-tech rooms.

Three years later, a follow-up report concluded that many of those recommendations, particularly the addition of more electronic equipment and the new schedule, had worked. But more alarm bells were raised: As the number of students in master’s and professional studies programs increased, classroom stock remained mostly unchanged.

“The major source of our problems is simple: we do not have enough classroom space to

accommodate our teaching needs,” the follow-up report said.

By 2017, another faculty report showed some departments still weren’t following the class scheduling guidelines developed years earlier.

“There is evidence that some units have taken these guidelines very seriously,” the report said at the time. “But a number of our course‐offering units do not seem to have done so yet.”
